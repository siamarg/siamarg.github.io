MEDICAL USE PRODUCTS

Hospitals produce massive quantities of waste. High-income countries contain a maximum of 0,5 kg of radioactive waste per hospital bed per day, according to the World Health Organization. However, this substance – which may be contagious, harmful or radioactive – only accounts for about 15% of the waste produced by healthcare. The other 85% is equivalent to household waste. It is a complicated method to deal with all the waste and hospitals dispose of it in many ways. Some incinerate their radioactive waste and give the remainder to waste disposal or recycling. However, some experts suggest that waste can be better handled if hospitals are converted to biodegradable – especially biodegradable – disposable goods. These could then be sent to anaerobic digestive plants or composting plant to produce a single waste stream that is more environmentally friendly.


The pandemic in COVID-19 has accelerated the world economy and disrupted the waste, plastics and recycling sectors. Although waste disposal, plastics and recycling seem only tangentially linked to essential services at first sight, they are closely tied to a thriving economy and vital public health functions. In India and beyond there have been major constraints on recycling and urban waste programs to the complexities related to the COVID- 19 pandemic. Meantime, due to global economic decreases, decreased collection rates and stoppages in bottle recovery plans where stock can only be integrated into the waste and recycling system until after the pandemic, the possible reduction in plastics waste production has been considerably reduced by the needs related to the pandemic. This leads in the disposal of further recyclables in conventional waste systems, deposition and incineration. The activity is further helped by a rapid drop in oil prices, which makes the production of recyclable materials cheaper. This contradicts environmental priorities but still reveals short-term and commodity alternatives to the problem of plastic waste while highlighting the need for a system-wide approach. PPE KITS & HAZMAT SUIT

Respirators, caps, facial shields, helmets, gowns, overalls, gloves and more are used with surgical PPE kits & HAZMAT suits, all of which are made of biodegradable plastic material and can be disposed without any worry of increasing the synthetic plastic pollution. For e.g., Respirators and surgical masks are usually made from polypropylene. As it takes up to 500 years for ordinary plastic objects made of identical materials to dissolve in the water, these masks could be around for a long time. Similarly, gloves are usually manufactured from inexpensive and sturdy plastics such as polyvinyl chloride (PVC) or Nitrile butadiene rubber (NBR), with a very long shelf life making them non-biodegradable.
